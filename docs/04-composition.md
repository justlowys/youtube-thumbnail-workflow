# 04 — Composition

Three layouts cover 95% of high-performing thumbnails. Pick one intentionally.

## 1. Symmetrical

Main subject centred. Left and right sides balanced — either mirrored or equally weighted. The face sits in the middle and elements wrap around it.

- **When to use**: strategy / framework / system videos where the creator IS the authority and the surrounding elements illustrate the system
- **Styles that use it**: `dense-whiteboard`, `cinematic-quote`
- **Stun gun combo**: large face + design collage + (optional) big text
- **Common mistake**: making the face too small so the collage dominates

Examples:
- Dense whiteboard with face in centre and flowcharts/funnels wrapping around
- Cinematic bookshelf portrait with face centred and a quoted headline at the bottom

## 2. Asymmetrical (Rule of Thirds)

Subject offset to one side — usually 1/3 from the left or right edge. The other 2/3 are visual space for a graphic, big text, or proof element.

- **When to use**: videos where the subject and the proof are equally important but distinct
- **Styles that use it**: left-face + right-graphic setups, single-finger pointing videos
- **Stun gun combo**: large face + visually compelling graphic (or big number)
- **Common mistake**: putting too much in the 2/3 empty space and losing the breathing room

Examples:
- Face on left, $100,000,000 bank balance card on the right
- Face on right, burning paper with a list on the left
- Face on left, pointing finger extending to a rising graph on the right

The "gold standard" pose for this layout: the subject's hand or finger points toward the graphic, creating a visual line from the face to the proof element.

## 3. A→B Split

Vertical split down the middle. Two distinct states, faces, or concepts on either side. The split itself is the visual element.

- **When to use**: comparison videos ("warm vs cold"), case studies (before/after), contrast videos ("wrong vs right")
- **Styles that use it**: `split-cold-warm`, case study thumbnails
- **Stun gun combo**: colour contrast (left red, right blue) + large face(s) + big text at the join
- **Common mistake**: making the two sides too visually similar so the split loses impact

Examples:
- Red left half / blue right half with face centred on the split
- Case study: client face left, creator face right, natural backgrounds, money banner at the bottom centre

## Where NOT to put important elements

- **Bottom-right corner**: YouTube's video duration overlay sits here. Anything you put in this corner is going to get covered by the timestamp.
- **Extreme left/right edges**: YouTube feed rendering can crop these in certain layouts (shelves, related videos).
- **Top 15%**: some feeds show the channel name or a progress bar at the top.

Keep important elements (face, main text, key numbers) in the central 80% of the frame.

## Scale check

Imagine the thumbnail at 1/16th size on an iPhone. At that scale:

- The face should still be recognizable as a face with an expression
- The largest text should still be readable
- The main graphic element should still be identifiable

If any of those three things fails, the element is too small or the composition is too cluttered. Scale up the dominant element, remove secondary elements, or increase the contrast.

## Colour hierarchy

Pick ONE dominant colour and use it everywhere important:

- Headline banner colour
- Accent shapes (circles, arrows, highlights)
- Brand bar / logo colour

Everything else should be neutral (white, black, grey) or a desaturated version of the dominant colour. Thumbnails with 4+ saturated colours look busy and unprofessional.

## The "face direction" trick

When using the asymmetrical layout with a pointing pose, the face should be looking AT the graphic, not away from it. The viewer's eye follows where the face is looking. If the face looks off-camera to the left and the graphic is on the right, the viewer's attention splits.

This is why the source photo matters so much. A good brand picture has the face angled toward the side where the graphic will live.

## When to break the rules

- **Split layout with 3+ elements**: sometimes a case study needs 3 people side by side. This is fine if each face is clearly separated and equally sized.
- **Symmetrical with intentional asymmetry**: e.g. face in centre but one dominant graphic on the left creates deliberate visual weight. Works for "stuck vs free" thumbnails.
- **Breaking the bottom-right rule**: sometimes the timestamp overlay IS the call-to-action. Rare but valid for certain hook videos.

Default to the 3 layouts above. Break them when you have a specific reason.
